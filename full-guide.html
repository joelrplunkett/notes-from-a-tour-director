Before You Go

Europe isn’t the kind of trip you just “figure out as you go”. You can. People do. But after running tours for years, you start to see what that actually looks like in real life.

What people think

“We’ll land, drop the bags, and work it out.”

What actually happens

The room isn’t ready, the luggage is annoying, and everyone is tired.

Why it matters

The first day sets the tone. Smooth or messy, you feel it.

Field Notes

People arrive exhausted, looking like zombies after their flight. No plan, no thought into how they’ll feel, no idea what to do when their room isn’t ready until 4pm and it’s 10am.

Maybe you feel fine. Great. But do you have everything you need to go out for a walk? Take a taxi somewhere for lunch? Sit somewhere? Grab a coffee without opening your suitcase in the hotel lobby?

Being prepared doesn’t lock you in. It makes you flexible.

The stuff brochures don’t show

Travel rarely runs perfectly. Delays, queues, waiting around, room not ready, room key not working, taxi taking forever. These things all suck.

But they’re part of travel. The social media posts, tour websites, and glossy brochures don’t capture that bit. Travel has ups and downs. The highs wouldn’t matter without the lows.

Expectation check

Paris syndrome is real. You picture quiet streets, perfect cafés, romantic meals near landmarks. Reality might be 35°C heat, crowds everywhere, construction, tour groups, and people trying to sell you frozen water bottles every ten seconds.

It’s not quite like the movies. But it can still be brilliant if you know what to expect beforehand.

Use This

Reality Check Before You Leave

Before you fly, run the boring-but-useful questions. They’re boring at home. They’re expensive and annoying once you’re there.

Arrival

What happens if your room isn’t ready for five hours?

Day bag

Can you comfortably go out without touching your checked luggage?

City pulse

Have you checked what’s on in the city that weekend?

Body

Can you walk 5,000–10,000 steps without it ruining your mood?

Bottom line: knowledge changes everything. Not more information. Just the stuff that actually makes the difference when you’re there.

The Pattern

After a while, you notice the same mistakes over and over again. Different people. Same outcome.

What people think

More planning means fewer mistakes.

What actually happens

They overplan the wrong things and underplan the important bits.

Why it matters

Energy goes into avoiding tiny mistakes while the big ones sneak through.

The mistake

Overthinking the wrong things

Hours spent choosing restaurants. Head in the phone, checking reviews, trying not to get ripped off, searching for the great deal. Time wasted trying to optimise. Sounds like an oxymoron.

That restaurant over the road from the Colosseum? Yeah, it might not be the best restaurant in Rome. But look at that real estate. You’re sitting outside, eating carbonara, looking up at thousands of years of arches and columns.

Is the price a rip-off? Not for a lunch you might remember for the rest of your life.

Field Notes

I see travel blogs say, “Don’t eat on the main square. Go a few streets back and eat like a local.” They’re not wrong. But locals live there. They see these things on the way to work. They didn’t fly 10,000 kilometres to see Rome.

An older Italian friend of mine goes to Bologna just to eat. He doesn’t look places up. He doesn’t ask his friends. He walks. He looks. He smells. Do people look happy? Are plates coming back empty? Does it smell good?

That’s the skill: knowing when to think with your head, and when to use your senses.

The other side

Underplanning the important bits

Arrival day. Travel days. How long things actually take. Google Maps says the drive is 45 minutes, but that assumes you know where you’re going, where to park, how toll roads work, whether there are traffic restrictions, and whether you’re even allowed to drive where the map is sending you.

You don’t. You’re figuring it out in real time.

Packing reality

It’s great to dress up and get nice photos. Of course. But those photos probably make up 1% of your trip. Don’t let the nice clothes take up 50% of your suitcase.

Europe means walking. Streets from the Middle Ages. Cars restricted. Coaches dropping you close, but not always at the door. In the months before your trip, think European: walk more, drive less. Stairs. Hills. Uneven ground. You don’t need to become a marathon runner. Just be comfortable moving.

Use This

The Planning Filter

Before spending another hour researching, ask whether this decision will actually change the trip.

Worth planning

Arrival day, luggage, transport, pace, hotel location, major timed entries.

Worth relaxing

Most lunches, café stops, short wandering time, the “perfect” photo spot.

Bottom line: most people don’t need more travel hacks. They need to stop optimising the wrong things.

Tour vs Independent

This is one of the biggest decisions you’ll make, and one of the most misunderstood. Tours aren’t good or bad. Independent travel isn’t good or bad. They’re tools.

What people think

Tour or independent is a personality test.

What actually happens

The wrong choice makes the whole trip harder than it should be.

Why it matters

You’re choosing between freedom, efficiency, responsibility, and mental space.

The shift

A tour is someone else handling the logistics

Forget the brochure for a second. A tour is transport sorted, hotels sorted, timing handled, route already figured out. You’re paying to take that stress away.

Control is great. We all want control. But with control comes responsibility. The question is whether you actually want to be responsible for everything that goes into executing a European trip.

Field Notes

Want to cook something new at home, let’s say a curry, and you’ve never cooked one before. Where do you start? Ingredients? Spices? Oils? Equipment? What temperature? What cooks first? How long? Does it look right?

Or do you order from a good restaurant and avoid the stress?

Am I saying it’s bad to learn? No. But when it’s your first curry, or you don’t do it often, let the people who know what they’re doing handle the stress and learn as you go.

Use This

How to Read a Tour Itinerary

Most people look at the places. That’s not enough. You need to look at the cadence.

Count nights

How many one-night stops? How many two-night stops? Where do you actually settle?

Check distance

Dots on a map look close. Berlin to Amsterdam still takes a full day with stops.

Compare pace

Five countries in eight days can work if you treat it like a taster, not a slow holiday.

Ask honestly

Do you want freedom, or do you want efficiency and less thinking?

Time is the value

After running these tours for years, I’ve realised that even with my knowledge and experience, there’s no way I could cover everything a good tour covers if I tried to replicate it on my own.

Arrive into Rome and visit the Colosseum that afternoon? On a tour, your driver drops you close, your luggage is safe on the coach, the visit has been arranged months ahead, and approved guides are ready. On your own, you’re thinking about the car, parking, luggage, tickets, timing, traffic restrictions, and how to get back out again.

Time is money. If a tour saves you a chunk of time and a chunk of stress, that’s the value.

Independent travel

More freedom. More flexibility. More responsibility. More room for mistakes.

Tours

Less freedom. More structure. Less thinking. More efficient use of time.

Quick Check

Should you book a tour?

Good fit

You have limited time, don’t enjoy logistics, want context, or it’s your first time.

Bad fit

You hate structure, want deep slow travel, or enjoy figuring things out yourself.

Bottom line: tours aren’t better. Independent travel isn’t better. The wrong choice is what hurts.

Trip Structure

This is where people really get it wrong. They build a list of places, then try to connect them, and mistake that for a trip.

What people think

Pick the places first. Connect them later.

What actually happens

The trip becomes packing, moving, catching up, and never settling.

Why it matters

Bad structure doesn’t look bad on paper. It feels bad when you’re in it.

The shift

A trip is not just where you go

A trip is how it flows. The cadence. How often you move. How often you pack and unpack. How much downtime you have. How tired you feel by day four or five. How much work you need to do to keep things going once you’re there.

Field Notes

Told yourself “I’ll sort that out once I’m there”? How likely are you going to feel like doing that after two or three days behind the steering wheel in a new place, or rushing from platform to platform on trains?

Think you’ll get that planning done on the train? What if it’s packed and loud? Kids, school groups, busy summer travel. Not every train ride is like that, but plenty are.

What you think you’ll manage later might not happen later.

Travel days are not empty space

You look at a map and think, “That’s only a few hundred kilometres.” Google says 2.5 hours.

What it actually is: packing bags, checking out, settling tabs, dropping keys, getting to the station or car, waiting, travelling, arriving, getting to the hotel, checking in, settling again.

What looks like 2.5 hours of travel can turn into a full day of energy.

Flight vs train reality

London to Paris. The flight might be 40 minutes. The train is around 90 minutes. You’d take the flight every time, right?

Well, think about getting to Heathrow from central London, arriving early for check-in and security, then landing at Charles de Gaulle and getting into Paris. Suddenly that 40 minute flight is a 6 hour travel experience.

The train might look more expensive than the flight, but St Pancras is central, Gare du Nord is central, and the whole experience can be smoother. Every case is different. That’s the point: you have to think about the whole day, not the headline time.

Use This

The Pace Test

Before you get excited by the map, test the structure.

Travel days

How many days are mostly movement, even if they don’t look like it?

One-night stops

How many times do you arrive, sleep, and leave?

Recovery

Where is the day that lets you catch up?

Day five

Will this still feel good when you’re not fresh anymore?

The silent killer

One-night stops

“Just one night in Venice.” “Just one night in Florence.” Sounds efficient.

What it actually feels like: arrive mid or late afternoon, rush to see something, dinner, back to hotel, pack again. You barely experience it. Worse, you never settle.

Simple framework

Build around pace

Fast

Lots of movement. More highlights. Treat it like a taster.

Medium

Balance. Some movement, some breathing room.

Slow

Fewer places. Deeper experience. More rhythm.

Rule

Start with how long you have, decide pace, then build around that.

Bottom line: you don’t regret not seeing one more place. You regret being too tired to enjoy the ones you were in.

Structuring a Day

People don’t usually ruin trips because they pick the wrong destinations. They ruin them with bad days — the stuff in between the highlights.

What people think

“We’ll do this in the morning, that in the afternoon, and something else at night.”

What actually happens

The day fills with movement, luggage, food problems, queues, and decision fatigue.

Why it matters

You don’t remember the spreadsheet. You remember how the day felt.

The real problem

Bad days derail good trips

People don’t ruin trips because they pick the wrong destinations.

They ruin them with bad days.

The things in between the destinations — the movement, the waiting, the rushing, the food, the luggage, the “we’ll just squeeze this in” thinking — that’s what quietly derails people.

You can bake a beautiful loaf of bread, slice it perfectly, and then ruin the sandwich by stuffing it with ham, pepperoni, chicken, tomato sauce, mayonnaise, mustard, onions and jam.

More filling doesn’t always make a better sandwich.

Same with travel days.

👉 A day is not just empty space waiting to be filled.

👉 It has a limit.

Field note: Paris to Lucerne

Paris to Lucerne sounds simple enough.

Breakfast in Paris, get away early, arrive in Lucerne by the afternoon. You’re moving from the French capital towards the Swiss Alps. Sounds beautiful. Sounds efficient.

On paper you start thinking: maybe something in the morning, travel, then a walk by the lake, then dinner.

Looks efficient.

Feels like chaos if you haven’t respected the day.

What people forget

An early start starts the night before

For starters, an early start means an early night.

If you’re driving or navigating the next day, that matters. Even if you’re taking public transport, there’s no magic coach outside the hotel with your name on it.

Someone has to go get the car, or you’re getting a taxi to the car, bus station or train station.

Bus leaves at 8:40?

You’ll want to be there early enough to make sure you’ve got the right station, the right platform, the right luggage plan, and enough buffer for the taxi actually showing up.

And Paris traffic at 8am? Good luck pretending that isn’t a factor.

Field note: The lunch problem

Okay, you made it. You’re on the way.

What about lunch?

Don’t tell me you were thinking of stealing from the breakfast room of the hotel.

I won’t tell anyone, but eating makeshift stolen soggy breakfast sandwiches gets old quick.

Most public transport doesn’t factor in a lunch stop. It runs when it runs. You sort out the rest.

Changing trains with 45 minutes between arrival and departure? Sounds like lunch. Then the first train is delayed five or ten minutes. Now you’re moving between platforms with luggage, looking for food, maybe needing the bathroom, touching every common surface known to mankind, and eating a rushed sandwich on the go.

Romantic? Not quite.

Field note: Arrival reality

You make it to the next city.

How are you feeling?

Relaxed? Prepared? Ready to enjoy the evening?

Maybe. But maybe not.

Public transport rarely organises itself around hotel check-in time. You’re either too early and now dealing with luggage storage, or later than expected and suddenly the afternoon has disappeared.

Dropping luggage at the hotel sounds easy until you realise you still have backpacks, valuables, passports, medication, chargers, and whatever else you don’t want disappearing from a storage room.

So now the route becomes transport hub → hotel → sightseeing → hotel to freshen up → back out again.

Depending where the hotel is, that may be fine.

Or it may quietly eat the day.

The hotel trade-off

Convenience costs money

Staying walking distance from everything sounds great.

It usually costs more.

Staying further out saves money.

Now taxis add up, public transport matters more, and going back out after a long travel day becomes a bigger decision.

And if you stay right beside the sights because it feels convenient, remember that other people also like being near those sights. At night. Early in the morning. With noise.

There is almost always a trade-off.

Field note: How tours make the same day feel different

How do tours do it then?

They don’t magically remove distance. They organise the day differently.

The hotel may be chosen with the route in mind. The drive is broken up properly. The coach becomes a controlled space. You’re not watching your suitcase at the end of a train carriage or wondering whether you’re on the right platform.

Your Tour Director keeps you informed of drive times, gives you enough context, then lets you relax.

By the time you arrive into Lucerne, you’ve been prepped. You know what’s happening. You can step off the coach and actually use your energy on the destination.

Then the driver stays with the coach, the heavier items can stay on board, and the check-in may already be handled. No lining up at reception with passports and credit cards while everyone is tired.

Am I biased towards tours? If they’re the right choice for you, yes.

If you’re prepared to deal with the moving parts of independent travel and allow time for them, then independent travel can be brilliant too.

That’s what this guide is about: opening the curtains on what you actually need to think about.

Core idea

Energy matters more than time

You don’t feel this at the start of the day.

You feel it at 3pm… when something simple suddenly feels like effort.

Morning

“Let’s add one more thing.”

Afternoon

“I can’t be bothered moving again.”

On paper

Travel looks clean and contained.

In reality

It bleeds into everything around it.

Field note: Food, sleep, routine

That assumes you’re eating properly, sleeping properly, and vaguely in a routine.

Living off croissants for breakfast, pasta for lunch and pizza for dinner catches up with you. Quickly.

Add planning, coordinating, adjusting, remembering what’s next — you’re going to feel it.

Use This

The Three-Part Day

For normal sightseeing days, don’t build a monster sandwich. Build a day that can breathe.

One anchor

The main thing the day is built around. Museum, major sight, guided visit, neighbourhood, or planned experience.

One flexible extra

Something nearby or easy that you can do if energy and timing allow.

Protected space

Food, rest, wandering, coffee, doing nothing, or just being where you are.

The rule

If everything depends on perfect timing, you have not planned a good day. You’ve planned a trap.

Between cities

How I’d plan a travel day

Don’t pretend travel days are sightseeing days.

If you’re moving between cities, that is the day.

Get from A to B properly. Save your energy for when you’re actually there.

Travel day rules

Public transport vs driving

Public transport

Conserve energy. Don’t rush. Don’t assume lunch, bathrooms, luggage storage and transfers will be easy.

Driving

Add time. For every hour on Google Maps, mentally add at least ten minutes, then plan proper stops.

Driving advantage

You can build in one good stop: one town, one sight, one proper break. Try to make it three and it gets messy.

Evening rule

Don’t plan much that night. You never quite know how the day will land.

Field note: Driving sounds simple until it isn’t

Driving gives you control, but it’s also a skill.

If you’re comfortable handling a vehicle on unfamiliar roads and routes, great. But motorway driving is only part of it.

Following GPS into cities you don’t know, finding parking, dealing with traffic restrictions, toll roads, signs, fuel, and then trying to enjoy a city approach while slightly tense — that all counts.

One proper stop can work beautifully.

Two starts to stretch things.

Three is usually fantasy unless stress is part of the hobby.

Bottom line: At the end of the day — pun intended — it’s not just what was in the day that matters. It’s how the day made you feel.

Stressed, rushed, good start, falling apart by the end?

Or informed, paced, fed, and still able to enjoy where you are?

Arrival Day

The first test, and one that can leave a mark. Not because of where you are — but because of how you land into it.

What people think

“We’ll land, drop the bags, freshen up and head out.”

What actually happens

You’re tired, the room isn’t ready, you’re carrying everything, and nothing feels simple.

Why it matters

The tone of your trip starts here. Smooth… or slightly off from the beginning.

The first reality

You don’t know how you’ll feel

It’s the morning at home, last-minute packing, getting to the airport, the airport itself, the flight, the lack of sleep, landing, immigration, luggage, arrivals hall.

Then suddenly:

👉 What now?

Field note

You’ll have a plan.

Most people do.

And it’s usually the same one:

👉 “We’ll just drop the bags and head out.”

Head out where?

Is the room ready?

Do you lie down? Stay up?

What time is it back home?

Room ready? The bed looks good.

Room not ready? Now you’re carrying everything.

Break it

What actually derails the day

  • Room not ready
  • Room smaller, dirtier, or worse than expected
  • No plan… or a plan too ambitious to even start
  • No energy
  • Carrying everything you own
Field note: The zombie phase

You don’t look like a zombie.

You feel like one.

The adrenaline gets you out of the airport.

Fresh air. New place. You made it.

Then it drops.

Small decisions start stacking up.

Where do we go?

Is this area fine?

Do we ask reception?

Do we just walk?

And that’s where it starts to wobble.

The fork

The invisible fork in the road

There are two types of arrival days.

  • Controlled → light, simple, intentional
  • Chaotic → forced, reactive, draining

You don’t choose this on the day.

You choose it in how you think about the day before you arrive.

Use This

The Arrival Framework

This is not the day to prove anything.

No big plans.

Adjust

Hydrate, listen to your body. Don’t crash early.

Nap rule

If you sleep, set an alarm. 45 minutes max.

Walk

Keep it light. No pressure. No expectations.

Security

Know where valuables are. Lock bags. Keep it simple.

  • Ask reception for a general direction, not “the best place”
  • Grab a hotel card or note the address
  • Check when food is available as a fallback
  • Pause, look at a map, memorise a couple of streets
  • Don’t walk out holding a map wide open

Go for a walk. No pressure.

You might find something great.

You might end up back at the hotel.

Both are fine.

Field note: Tours vs independent

Tours feel easier on Day 1 for a reason.

Morning start? You arrived the night before and naturally rested.

Afternoon or evening start? You hit the ground running, but with structure already in place.

You know where to be and when before you even leave home.

Your Tour Director reaches out. There’s clarity.

Sometimes rooms are ready earlier because of existing relationships.

You’ve got someone to ask. Someone visible. Someone who knows the area.

Then you meet the group. Energy lifts.

No hiding in the room. No drifting into a bad sleep pattern.

Dinner, introductions, structure — then rest.

Bottom line: Arrival day is not where you prove anything.

It’s where you avoid digging a hole you spend two days climbing out of.

Ease into it.

No big plans.
